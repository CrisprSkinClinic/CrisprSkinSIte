One of the most common questions we hear isn't "how much does a hair transplant cost" -- it's "am I actually a good candidate for one." That's the right question to lead with, and the honest answer is: it depends on a few specific factors, not just how much hair you've lost.

## Donor Density Matters More Than People Expect

The donor area -- typically the back and sides of the scalp -- has a finite, genetically determined supply of permanent hair. A transplant redistributes this hair; it doesn't create new hair follicles. If donor density is limited relative to the area needing coverage, overharvesting to chase a higher graft count can permanently reduce future donor supply, which matters if hair loss continues to progress elsewhere on the scalp and a second session is ever needed.

## Stability of Hair Loss Is Just as Important

If your hair loss is still actively progressing rather than stable, we often recommend medical therapy first to slow it down before considering surgery. Transplanting hair into an area while loss continues around it can produce a result that looks less natural over time as the surrounding native hair continues to thin.

## Age Isn't the Deciding Factor -- Pattern Stability Is

There's no fixed minimum age for a hair transplant. What matters more is whether your specific pattern of loss has stabilized enough that surgical planning can account for how it's likely to progress. In younger patients with early, still-evolving hair loss, this often means starting with medical treatment and revisiting surgery later.

## The Honest Bottom Line

A proper evaluation looks at your donor density, the stability and pattern of your hair loss, and your realistic goals -- and sometimes the right answer is "not yet, let's stabilize things first" rather than proceeding straight to surgery. That's not us being overly cautious; it's what actually produces a result that holds up well over the years that follow.
