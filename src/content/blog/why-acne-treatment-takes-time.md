One of the most common reasons acne treatment doesn't work isn't that the treatment itself was wrong -- it's that it was abandoned before it had a real chance to work.

## The Six-to-Eight-Week Rule

Most acne treatments, whether topical or oral, need six to eight weeks of consistent use before you can fairly judge whether they're working. Skin cell turnover and the processes acne treatments target simply operate on that kind of timeline. Expecting visible improvement in a week sets you up to abandon something that would have worked.

## Why Switching Products Constantly Backfires

We often see patients who've tried five different products in two months, each for a week or two, because none of them seemed to be "doing anything." In most cases, none of them had been given enough time to actually work, and constantly switching also increases the risk of irritation, which can look like a new problem layered on top of the original acne.

## Acne Type Matters for Treatment Choice

Acne isn't one uniform condition -- comedonal acne (blackheads and whiteheads), inflammatory acne (papules and pustules), and cystic or nodular acne all respond to somewhat different treatment approaches. Using a mismatched product for your specific acne type is another common reason treatment feels like it's not working.

## The Case for an Evaluation

An evaluation identifies your specific acne type and severity, which shapes what's actually likely to work and sets a realistic timeline from the outset -- so you know what to expect and aren't tempted to abandon an effective treatment plan too early.
