Birthmarks are extremely common and come in several distinct types -- including vascular birthmarks (such as salmon patches and infantile haemangiomas) and pigmented birthmarks (such as congenital moles and Mongolian spots) -- each with a different natural course. Many fade or resolve on their own over months to a few years without any treatment at all, while a smaller number benefit from monitoring or, in specific circumstances, treatment.

The single most valuable thing an evaluation provides for most parents is accurate reassurance: correctly identifying the type of birthmark your child has, and understanding its typical natural course, resolves most of the anxiety that brings families in for a first visit.

## What to Expect at Your Visit

We examine the birthmark to classify its specific type, noting size, location, and any change since you first noticed it. Based on the type identified, we explain the expected natural course -- whether it's likely to fade, stay the same, or occasionally grow before eventually resolving -- and what, if anything, would be a reason to bring your child back sooner than a routine follow-up.

## Treatment Approach

For the majority of birthmarks, which are expected to resolve naturally over time, our approach is structured monitoring rather than intervention -- with clear guidance on exactly what changes (rapid growth, ulceration, bleeding, or specific patterns depending on type) would warrant a prompt follow-up rather than waiting for a scheduled visit.

For birthmarks where treatment is genuinely indicated -- based on size, location near a sensitive structure like the eye or airway, or a growth pattern that suggests it won't resolve favorably on its own -- we discuss appropriate options and coordinate with other specialists where needed.

## Setting Realistic Expectations

Most birthmarks are cosmetic and medically inconsequential, and the honest, most helpful thing we can offer for these is accurate information and appropriate reassurance rather than unnecessary treatment. For the smaller number of birthmarks that do need monitoring or treatment, we explain clearly why, and what the realistic course of action and expected outcome looks like for your child's specific case.
