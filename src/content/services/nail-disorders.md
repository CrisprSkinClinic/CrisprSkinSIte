Nail changes -- discoloration, thickening, crumbling, pitting, or separation from the nail bed -- have a range of possible causes, most commonly fungal infection, but also including nail psoriasis, prior trauma, and less commonly other systemic conditions. Because these look similar to the untrained eye but require quite different treatment, an accurate diagnosis is the most important first step, particularly since nail treatment courses run considerably longer than skin treatment and getting the diagnosis wrong wastes months.

Ingrown nails are a separate, mechanical problem -- usually affecting the big toe -- caused by the nail edge growing into the surrounding skin, leading to pain, swelling, and sometimes infection if left untreated.

## What to Expect at Your Visit

We examine the affected nail or nails closely, noting the specific pattern of change, since fungal infection, psoriasis, and trauma each tend to produce somewhat different nail changes. Where fungal infection is suspected, a nail clipping or scraping sent for microscopy or culture confirms the diagnosis before committing to a long treatment course -- an important step, since oral antifungal treatment isn't something to start on assumption alone.

## Treatment Approach

Fungal nail infections are treated with topical antifungal therapy for early or limited involvement, or oral antifungal treatment for more established infection -- and we're upfront that nail treatment takes patience: fingernails take months and toenails can take the better part of a year to grow out fully, so visible improvement is gradual by nature, not a sign of ineffective treatment.

Ingrown nails are managed conservatively where possible, with a minor in-clinic procedure reserved for recurrent or more severe cases. Where nail changes are traced to psoriasis or another underlying condition, treatment is coordinated as part of managing that condition overall, since treating the nail in isolation without addressing the cause tends to be less effective.

## Setting Realistic Expectations

Nail growth is slow, and this is the single most important thing to understand before starting nail treatment -- a fungal nail infection that's been present for a year took that long to develop and will take proportionate time to grow out clear, even with fully effective treatment. We set this timeline expectation clearly at your first visit so progress is judged accurately rather than assumed to be failing.
