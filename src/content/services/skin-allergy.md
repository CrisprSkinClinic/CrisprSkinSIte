Skin allergies -- more precisely, allergic and irritant contact dermatitis -- occur when the skin reacts to a specific substance it has come into contact with, ranging from cosmetics and jewellery to plants, fragrances, and certain fabrics. The reaction pattern often reflects where and how contact occurred, which is itself a useful diagnostic clue, but pinning down the exact culprit usually takes a systematic approach rather than guesswork.

Distinguishing a contact allergy from other forms of dermatitis or eczema matters because the core solution is different: identifying and avoiding the specific trigger, rather than managing an ongoing internal pattern the way atopic eczema is typically managed.

## What to Expect at Your Visit

We examine the pattern, location, and appearance of the reaction, which often gives clues about the likely category of trigger -- a reaction limited to the earlobes or wrist, for instance, points differently than one affecting broader areas. We take a detailed history of recent new products, jewellery, occupational exposures, and the timing of reactions relative to specific activities or product use.

Where a specific contact allergen is suspected but not obvious from history alone, patch testing -- a systematic test applying small amounts of common allergens to the skin -- can help identify the exact trigger with more certainty than guesswork.

## Treatment Approach

The current reaction is treated with topical anti-inflammatory therapy to settle inflammation and itching. In parallel, we work through your history and any patch testing results to identify the likely trigger, since resolving a contact allergy long-term depends far more on avoidance than on ongoing treatment.

Once a trigger is identified, we provide specific, practical guidance -- including how to read ingredient labels for known allergens under their various naming conventions, which is often where people get tripped up even after they think they've identified and eliminated the culprit.

## Setting Realistic Expectations

Most contact reactions resolve well once the trigger is identified and avoided. Where no clear single trigger emerges despite testing -- which does happen -- we discuss a broader approach to reducing exposure to common irritant categories while continuing to manage symptoms as needed.
