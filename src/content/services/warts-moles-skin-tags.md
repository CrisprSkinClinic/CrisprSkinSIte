Warts, moles, and skin tags are all common, generally benign skin growths, but they're different in cause and, importantly, in when removal is actually medically relevant versus purely a matter of personal preference. Warts are caused by a viral infection of the skin and are contagious; skin tags are harmless growths of skin, more common with age, friction, and certain metabolic factors; moles are pigmented growths that are usually harmless but occasionally warrant closer attention if they change.

We start every visit with an honest assessment of what we're actually looking at, since not every growth benefits from removal, and unnecessary removal carries its own small risks (scarring, in particular) that aren't worth taking for something that was never a concern.

## What to Expect at Your Visit

We examine the growth or growths in question, often using dermoscopy -- a handheld magnification tool -- for a closer look, particularly for pigmented lesions. For moles specifically, we assess against the recognized warning signs: asymmetry, irregular borders, multiple or uneven colors, a diameter larger than a pencil eraser, and any recent evolution in size, shape, or color. Where a lesion's nature isn't clear on examination alone, a biopsy provides a definitive answer.

## Treatment Approach

Warts typically respond to cryotherapy (freezing), often requiring a few sessions spaced a few weeks apart depending on size and location. Skin tags and most benign moles that a patient wants removed for comfort or cosmetic reasons are removed surgically or via electrosurgery under local anaesthesia, a quick in-clinic procedure.

Where any lesion shows features that raise even a modest question, we recommend biopsy for pathology confirmation before proceeding further -- this is a small, low-risk step that provides real certainty, and we don't skip it to save time.

## When Removal Is (and Isn't) Necessary

Most skin tags and cosmetically bothersome moles are removed simply because the patient wants them gone -- that's a perfectly valid reason, and we're happy to help. But we're equally direct when a growth doesn't need removal at all, since minor surgical procedures, however low-risk, are still procedures, and we'd rather tell you it's unnecessary than perform a removal that wasn't actually indicated.
